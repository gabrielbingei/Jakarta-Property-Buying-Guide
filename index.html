00  /  The short version

Five things that decide everything else

If you read nothing else on this page, read this. Every recommendation further down is downstream of these five facts.

  1. The city slopes, and so do the prices. South Jakarta sits on higher, firmer ground. North Jakarta is alluvial coast, parts of it below sea level, subsiding several centimetres a year. That gradient, not fashion, is the spine of the price map.
  2. Land appreciates. Buildings depreciate. Strata title does both badly. Landed housing has outperformed apartments consistently, and Indonesian resale demand strongly favours land. An apartment is a lifestyle and cashflow decision; a house is a wealth decision.
  3. Transit is the only thing rezoning the map. Under the current Jakarta detailed spatial plan, allowable building intensity is scored on the infrastructure serving a site. A new station literally increases what you may build. Rail access is now a pricing variable, not just a convenience.
  4. The headline price is roughly 90% of the cheque. Budget 8–15% on top for transfer duty, notary, financing and fit-out. See the calculator.
  5. Nobody credible is forecasting a boom. Realistic 2026 growth: 0–2% for weak apartment stock, 2–5% for good landed, 4–6% in the strongest Greater Jakarta townships. Buy for use and yield, not for a flip.

What this document is. A structured synthesis of public market research (Colliers, Cushman & Wakefield, JLL, Bank Indonesia, BPS), Indonesian property law, and infrastructure plans, assembled into a decision tool. Price bands marked indicative are judgement calls built from asking-price ranges — treat them as a starting bracket for negotiation, not as an appraisal. It is not legal or financial advice, and I am not a licensed advisor. Before you sign anything, get a notary/PPAT and, if you're financing, an independent appraiser.

01  /  Framing the decision

Apartment or house? Buy or rent?

These are two separate questions and most buyers collapse them into one. Answer them in order.

Question one: are you buying an asset, or a life?

Landed house

Buy this if you want wealth

You own land, which is the only part of Indonesian residential property that reliably appreciates. Resale demand is deep because the cultural preference for land ownership is genuine and durable. Greater Jakarta land prices have been moving ~3% a year, against ~1% for apartments.

The cost: in the zones you'd actually want, a house is a 15–60 billion rupiah decision. Outside them, you are buying a long commute. Maintenance is on you. Security is on you.

Apartment / strata

Buy this if you want a life

You buy proximity, security, facilities and zero maintenance. In central Jakarta it's the only realistic way to live inside the traffic ring. Foreigners can own strata; they cannot own freehold land.

The cost: you own a share of a building, not land. Service charges (IPL) and sinking-fund calls escalate. Older, poorly managed towers are the single worst-performing asset class in this market right now. Resale is slow: apartments sit on market roughly 95 days versus ~70 for houses.

Question two: does buying beat renting for you?

Jakarta's price-to-income ratio sits around 15–20× annual household income for formal housing — comparable to Bangkok or Manila, well below Singapore. Gross rental yields run roughly 5–8%, which is respectable, but mortgage rates and the cautious lending environment eat much of that spread.

The rent-first rule. If either of you has any realistic chance of leaving Jakarta inside five years, rent. Transaction costs in and out are roughly 8–15% on purchase and another ~2.5% final income tax on sale (paid by the seller). At 0–3% annual appreciation, a sub-five-year hold is a near-certain loss in real terms. Buying makes sense on a seven-year-plus horizon, or if the property is genuinely for keeps.

Question three: how much house can you carry?

The bank will lend on your income; that isn't the same as what you should borrow. Two guardrails worth holding:

  • Debt serviceKeep total monthly debt (KPR + everything else) at or under 30–35% of combined net monthly income. Indonesian banks will often approve above this. Don't take it.
  • Cash bufferDo not spend your last rupiah on the down payment. Hold 6 months of full instalments plus service charges in reserve, in cash, after closing. Property is illiquid here — a forced sale in a soft market is the trap.
  • CurrencyIf either income is in a foreign currency and the loan is in rupiah, you carry FX risk in both directions. Rupiah weakness in 2026 has already bitten. Size the loan against your rupiah-equivalent income at a conservative rate, not today's rate.

The pattern to notice. Tier 1 addresses are scarce and defensible but flat — you're paying for permanence. The growth is in two places only: the transit-upzoned nodes (Lebak Bulus, Fatmawati, Cipete, and eventually Tomang and the Thamrin–Kota Tua axis), and the townships (BSD, Serpong). Everything else is holding its value, not building it.

    04  /  What it actually costs

    The cheque is bigger than the price

    Indonesian property purchases carry roughly 8–15% in costs on top of the headline. Here is every line, and a calculator that adds them up.

    Every line item

    • BPHTB5% of the transaction value, minus the regional threshold deduction (NPOPTKP). Buyer pays. This is the big one.
    • PPh final2.5% of the transaction value. Seller pays — but on a new-build from a developer, assume it's baked into your price.
    • PPN (VAT)11–12% on new-build purchases from a developer (PKP). Currently heavily subsidised: the government's VAT-borne-by-government scheme (PPN DTP) runs through 2026 with signals of extension to Dec 2027, covering landed houses up to IDR 5bn with a subsidy cap on the first IDR 2bn. Secondary/resale purchases from an individual are not subject to VAT.
    • Notary / PPAT0.5–1%, negotiable. Covers the AJB (deed of sale), title search, and transfer registration at the BPN land office. Do not let the seller pick the notary.
    • Bank / KPR fees~1% provision fee, plus appraisal, administration, life and fire insurance. Adds up to ~1.5–2.5% of the loan.
    • Agent2–3%, normally the seller's cost, but confirm.
    • Fit-outThe line everyone forgets. A bare "hand-over" unit needs flooring, kitchen, AC, wardrobes. Budget IDR 3–8m per sqm depending on finish.
    • Ongoing: IPLApartment service charge, typically IDR 15,000–30,000 per sqm per month plus a sinking fund. On a 150 sqm unit that's IDR 2.25–4.5m every month, forever, and it escalates. Model it.
    • Ongoing: PBBAnnual land & building tax. Modest, but it scales with the assessed value (NJOP) which the city periodically re-rates upward.

    The KPR teaser-rate trap. Indonesian banks advertise a low fixed rate for the first 1–3 years, then float. The floating rate is what you will actually pay for most of the loan, and it moves with the BI rate — which rose in June 2026. Model your affordability at the floating rate plus 2%, never at the teaser. If it only works at the teaser, it doesn't work.

    05  /  Title & law

    What you are actually allowed to own

    In Indonesia the certificate on the title, not the building, determines what you have. Get this wrong and nothing else matters.

    SHM

    Hak Milik — freehold

    The strongest right. Perpetual, inheritable, mortgageable. Indonesian citizens only. This is what you want for a landed house.

    HGB

    Hak Guna Bangunan

    Right to build, typically 30 years, extendable 20, renewable another 30. Available to Indonesian citizens and to PT (Indonesian companies, including PMA). Most apartments and most developer housing sits on HGB. Renewable in practice, but it is a renewal, with fees, not a right in perpetuity.

    SHMSRS

    Strata title

    Your apartment unit. It is a share of a building sitting on the underlying HGB (or HGB over HPL). Check when the underlying HGB expires — on a 1990s tower, that clock is well advanced, and renewal is a collective problem.

    Hak Pakai

    Right of use

    The route for foreign individuals holding a valid residence permit. Applies to a landed house on Hak Pakai land, or to an apartment.

    HGB / HPL

    The one to watch

    HGB granted over state or municipal management rights (HPL). Renewal is not automatic and depends on the HPL holder. Common in reclaimed and redeveloped areas. Ask the notary explicitly.

    PPJB / AJB

    Contract vs. deed

    PPJB is only a binding sale agreement — common on off-plan. Ownership does not transfer until the AJB is executed before a PPAT and registered at the BPN. Do not treat a PPJB as ownership.

    If either of you is a foreign national

    • ApartmentsPermitted, via Hak Pakai / strata, with a valid stay permit. In Jakarta there is a minimum purchase price of roughly IDR 3 billion, which in practice pushes foreign buyers into two-bedroom units and above.
    • LandForeigners cannot hold Hak Milik. Full stop.
    • The nominee structureBuying in an Indonesian friend's or spouse's name to hold freehold is widely done and legally void. The nominee is the legal owner; side agreements have been struck down. The most-regretted mistake foreign buyers make here is getting the land right wrong. Do not do it.
    • Mixed marriageWithout a prenuptial (or postnuptial) agreement separating assets, an Indonesian spouse's ability to hold Hak Milik can be compromised. If this is your situation, see a notary before you shop, not after.

    The single highest-value hour you will spend. Take the certificate number to the BPN land office (or have your notary do a pengecekan sertifikat) and verify: the registered owner matches the seller, the land area matches the drawing, the expiry date, and whether it is encumbered by a mortgage (hak tanggungan) or a court block (sita). Do this before you pay any deposit. A deposit paid on an unverified certificate is money you may simply not get back.

    06  /  Physical risk

    Water is the permanent variable

    Jakarta is subsiding. Roughly 40% of the city sits below sea level, and parts of the north are sinking on the order of several centimetres a year — in places far more. This is not a weather problem, it is a geology problem, and it is the deepest structural discount in the market.

    What to check on any specific property

    • Ask the neighboursNot the agent. Ask the satpam, the warung owner, the RT/RW head: "how high did the water come in 2020? In 2007?" People remember, and they will tell you honestly.
    • Look at the drivewaysIf houses on the street have raised entrances, added steps, or fresh render at ankle height, the street floods. This is the cheapest survey you will ever run.
    • Go in the wet seasonRoughly October to April. Viewing a Jakarta property only in the dry season is how people buy a swimming pool.
    • Check the drainWhich kali (canal) does your street drain into, and how far is it? Kemang's flooding is a drainage-catchment story, not a coastal one.
    • GroundwaterIf the building extracts its own groundwater, it is contributing to its own subsidence. Ask what the water source is. Piped supply is a quality signal.

    The Giant Sea Wall question

    A ~19 km barrier across Jakarta Bay is targeted to break ground around September 2026, at an estimated USD 8–10 billion over 8–10 years, as one segment of a 575 km, 15-segment national programme along Java's north coast — total budget floated around USD 80 billion.

    Every North Jakarta valuation is implicitly a bet on this. If it gets built, the north's flood discount compresses and PIK's premium is validated. If it stalls — and USD 80 billion is a number that has slipped before — subsidence keeps eating the coast.

    Our position on this: do not pay a sea-wall premium for property that only makes sense if the sea wall is built. If you want north exposure, buy inside PIK's existing polder system, which already works, rather than in Sunter or Ancol, which are waiting.

    What the consultants are actually saying

    • ColliersThe market "stabilized" through 2025 — not booming, not under pressure. South Jakarta is the most active corridor and is expected to supply ~60% of 2026's new units. Under-construction transactions are concentrated in the high-end luxury segment. The property type most likely to underperform is the old or poorly managed apartment: service charges, oversupply and weak resale eat the returns.
    • Cushman & WakefieldProjects connected to the MRT, LRT and KRL recorded better sales than other locations. The Thamrin–Kota Tua corridor is the next development hotspot. Landed housing is showing resilience.
    • Expat housingDemand is replacement-led, not growth-led. New supply is constrained by land scarcity in mature expat neighbourhoods, so secure, well-managed compounds retain pricing power, and competition for premium family units stays intense through 2026. Family-sized (3BR+) stock in established expat districts is genuinely scarce.
    • Forecast2026: 0–2% growth in weaker apartment locations, 2–5% in good landed, 4–6% in the strongest Greater Jakarta neighbourhoods (BSD, Gading Serpong, Alam Sutera, Bintaro, Cilandak, Cipete). Headwinds: the June 2026 BI rate rise, weak household purchasing power, and pockets of local oversupply.

    09  /  Before you sign

    The checklist

    Work top to bottom. Do not skip 01, and do not pay a deposit before 04.

    Title and legal

    1. Run a certificate check at BPNHave the notary/PPAT do a pengecekan sertifikat. Verify the owner, the area, the expiry, and any mortgage or court block. Before any money moves.
    2. Read the expiry date on the HGBEspecially on any building older than ~1995. Ask who is responsible for renewal and what it will cost. On strata, this is a collective liability you're inheriting.
    3. Confirm PBG / IMB existsThe building permit. A house with unpermitted extensions is a house you cannot cleanly sell or finance.
    4. Appoint your own notaryNot the seller's, not the developer's. This is the person protecting your money.
    5. Check the zoning (ITBX)Look the plot up against the RDTR — the Jakarta Satu portal has an interactive map. Confirm the residential use is permitted and see the allowed intensity. It also tells you what your neighbour may one day build.

    The asset itself

    1. Visit in the wet season and at rush hourTwice. Once when it rains. Once at 7:30am on a Tuesday. Time the actual journey to work and to school.
    2. For apartments: audit the building, not the unitAsk for the management's financials. What is the IPL per sqm, how much has it risen in five years, what is in the sinking fund, and when were the lifts, pumps and chillers last replaced? A tower with a depleted sinking fund is a future special assessment with your name on it.
    3. Check the occupancy rate of the towerWalk the corridors at night and count lit windows. A half-empty tower has a broken service-charge model and a broken resale market.
    4. Research the developerLook at what they delivered five and ten years ago, and whether they delivered on time. On off-plan, this is the whole investment.
    5. Get an independent valuationNot the bank's, not the agent's. Especially in a market where 70–80% of stock sells below asking.
    6. NegotiateMedian discount to asking is ~4%. Days on market are stretching. In this market, the first number is not the number.

    10  /  Worked example

    Case study: Somerset Berlian

    A concrete application of everything above — and a useful illustration of how a good address and a good asset are not the same thing.

    • LocationJl. Permata Berlian, Grogol Utara, Kebayoran Lama, South Jakarta 12210 — the Permata Hijau district.
    • Asset104 serviced apartment units, 2–3 bedroom, family-sized. Developed by PT Permata Berlian Realty, a Pakuwon subsidiary. Early/mid-1990s vintage.
    • Indicative resaleAsks around IDR 2.7bn have circulated for larger units.
    • AmenityPool, gym, sauna, tennis, playground, minimart, restaurant, 24h security. Near ITC Permata Hijau, Gandaria City, Senayan; Binus and JIS within reach.

    The read

    What's good: Permata Hijau is genuinely leafy and well-planned, with real cachet and permanent land scarcity. 3-bedroom family stock in an established expat district is exactly the thing consultants say is structurally short. Pakuwon is a competent developer. The expat rental pool here is replacement-led and stable.

    What's not: three things, and they compound.

    — It sits in Kebayoran Lama, not Baru. On the cheaper side of a 1950s planning line, on the congested Jl. Panjang corridor.
    — No MRT. In a market where transit-connected stock is measurably outperforming, this is a handicap that will not fix itself.
    — 1990s serviced apartment, non-CBD. That is, precisely, the product category Colliers flags as the likeliest underperformer — and the CBD premium over the periphery has been widening.

    Verdict. A hold-and-let asset, not an appreciation asset. The scarcity of large expat-grade family units is the floor under it; the age of the building and the absence of rail are the ceiling. If you're considering it, the questions that decide it are all in the building's accounts: what is the IPL per sqm, how fast is it rising, what's in the sinking fund, when does the underlying HGB expire, and who pays for the renewal? Ask those four questions before you ask about the view.

    The comparable set to price it against

    Do not value it against the market. Value it against these:

    • PakubuwonoThe local premium benchmark in the same district. Sets the ceiling. If Somerset trades at a wide discount to Pakubuwono per sqm, ask why — and then check whether the answer is fixable (finishes) or not (building age, management).
    • Pondok Indah Golf AptsSame product, same era, same tenant pool — but with JIS on the doorstep and a golf outlook. The relevant question: what is the school-proximity premium worth, and are you paying it?
    • Senayan / Sudirman servicedThe CBD comparison. This is the yardstick that tells you what the non-CBD discount is currently worth. It has been widening.
    • Cipete / Fatmawati new stockThe transit alternative. Newer, smaller, on the MRT. If growth matters more to you than space, this is the honest competitor.
